Purpose of the study

Deepfake content is increasingly common online and is frequently used for misinformation, manipulation, and fraud[cite: 2]. As deepfake images grow more realistic, understanding how people tell authentic from manipulated visuals is essential[cite: 2].

This study investigates how people distinguish deepfakes from genuine images using eye-tracking and self-reports[cite: 2]. It explores whether visual patterns and reasoning influence detection, hypothesising that cues aid recognition and that brief education improves accuracy[cite: 2].

Why have I been chosen?

You are invited to participate in this study because you meet the inclusion criteria[cite: 2]:

  • Participants must be aged 18 years or older[cite: 2]
  • Individuals who have normal vision sufficient for viewing images and completing eye-tracking calibration[cite: 2]
  • Individuals who do not have cognitive difficulties
  • Individuals who can read and understand English[cite: 2]

These criteria are necessary for full and effective participation of designed activities of the study[cite: 2].

Do I have to take part?

Taking part is entirely voluntary[cite: 2]. You are free to decide whether or not to take part[cite: 2]. If you do decide to take part, you will be asked to give your consent via a Qualtrics survey[cite: 2]. You are still free to withdraw at any time after giving your consent and without giving a reason[cite: 2]. A decision to withdraw, or a decision not to take part, will not affect you in any way[cite: 2]. Refusal or withdrawal will involve no penalty or loss, now or in the future[cite: 2].

What will happen to me if I take part?

If you agree to take part, you will complete an online survey through this dedicated website[cite: 2], which will proceed as follows:

Structure of the Study:

  • Eye-tracker Calibration: Calibration to allow accurate recording of your gaze patterns and areas of visual attention[cite: 2].
  • Classification Task 1: You will view 10 images (a mix of deepfake and non-deepfake images) and classify each as either "deepfake" or "not deepfake"[cite: 2]. Eye-tracking data will be recorded throughout the image viewing tasks[cite: 2].
  • Follow-up Questions: After each image, you will be asked a classification question ("Was the image shown a deepfake image?") and an open-ended question ("How can you tell that the image was or was not a deepfake?") to justify your decision[cite: 2].
  • Educational Intervention: You will watch a short video highlighting common indicators of deepfake images[cite: 2].
  • Classification Task 2: You will view a new set of 10 images and repeat the classification and explanation process[cite: 2].
  • Final Questions & Debrief: A short concluding section and written debriefing[cite: 2].

What do I have to do?

There are no lifestyle restrictions as a result of participating[cite: 2]. You are simply asked to view the images naturally and complete the questionnaire segments.

Are there possible disadvantages and/or risks in taking part?

The disadvantages and/or risks in taking part are considered low and comparable to those encountered in everyday life. However, the study could potentially induce low to mild psychological stress, frustration, or anxiety beyond the risks encountered in normal life, especially when trying to differentiate difficult images[cite: 2]. You are completely free to withdraw without giving any reasons at any point if you feel uncomfortable[cite: 2].

What are the possible benefits of taking part?

By taking part in the study, you will learn more about deepfake facial images and how to better distinguish them from real ones. Such skills can help you avoid becoming a victim of online harms generated by deepfake facial images. Beyond the benefits for yourself, your participation will help the research team understand the effectiveness of training on deepfake detection, which can guide digital literacy efforts[cite: 2].

Will my taking part in this project be kept confidential?

Information collected during this study will be held confidentially by the researchers in line with the UK Data Protection Act 2018[cite: 2]. Only researchers involved in the study will be authorized to access the data, but anonymised responses may be shared with other researchers or made available in online data repositories[cite: 2].

To ensure confidentiality of your data, your data files will not include identifying personal information (or you will use a unique participant identification number)[cite: 2]. Any identifying details will be stored separately and securely[cite: 2]. All anonymized data will be held securely on University of Kent computers or by approved off-site data collection partners (such as Qualtrics)[cite: 2]. Any publication resulting from this work will report only aggregated findings or fully anonymized examples[cite: 2].

What will happen to the results of the research project?

The results of the research are being collected as part of a Masters Project[cite: 2]. Findings are expected to be published or presented at scientific venues[cite: 2]. If any individual data items are presented, they will be totally anonymous, without any means of identifying the individuals involved[cite: 2]. A summary of the results will be available from the researcher on request[cite: 2].
